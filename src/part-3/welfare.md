Welfare economics provides a framework for evaluating the efficiency and distributional consequences of market outcomes and policy interventions.

## Consumer & Producer Surplus

**Consumer surplus (CS)** is the difference between willingness to pay and the price actually paid — the area above the price line and below the demand curve.

**Producer surplus (PS)** is the difference between the price received and the minimum price a seller would accept — the area below the price line and above the supply curve.

**Total surplus = CS + PS**. A competitive equilibrium maximizes total surplus; this is the first welfare theorem.

## Efficiency vs. Equity

A market outcome can be efficient (maximizing total surplus) while also being deeply inequitable. Welfare economics distinguishes between:

- **Pareto efficiency** — no one can be made better off without making someone worse off
- **Kaldor-Hicks efficiency** — total gains exceed total losses (gainers could in principle compensate losers)

Most policy debates involve trading off efficiency for equity or vice versa.

## Deadweight Loss

Any policy that moves the market away from the competitive equilibrium (a tax, a price ceiling, a quota) typically creates **deadweight loss** — a reduction in total surplus that benefits no one. Measuring DWL is central to cost-benefit analysis of policy interventions.
