An externality occurs when the actions of a buyer or seller affect third parties who are not part of the market transaction. Externalities cause markets to produce outcomes that are not socially optimal.

## Negative Externalities

When production or consumption imposes costs on third parties (e.g., pollution), the social cost exceeds the private cost. Markets overproduce relative to the social optimum.

**Policy responses:**
- **Pigouvian tax** — set equal to the marginal external cost at the optimum
- **Cap-and-trade** — issue tradeable permits up to the efficient quantity
- **Regulation** — mandate a maximum level of the harmful activity

## Positive Externalities

When production or consumption generates benefits for third parties (e.g., vaccinations, education), the social benefit exceeds the private benefit. Markets underproduce.

**Policy responses:**
- **Pigouvian subsidy** — subsidize by the amount of the marginal external benefit
- **Public provision** — government provides the good directly

## The Coase Theorem

If property rights are well-defined and transaction costs are zero, private parties can bargain to an efficient outcome regardless of the initial assignment of rights. In practice, transaction costs often prevent this.
