Perfect competition is the benchmark market structure in microeconomics. It describes a market with many buyers and sellers, homogeneous products, free entry and exit, and perfect information.

## Characteristics

- **Price-taking behavior** — no individual firm can influence the market price
- **Zero economic profit in the long run** — free entry drives profits to zero
- **Allocative efficiency** — P = MC, so resources are allocated to their highest-valued uses
- **Productive efficiency** — firms produce at minimum average cost

## Short-Run Equilibrium

In the short run, a competitive firm maximizes profit by setting **P = MC**. It will continue operating as long as P ≥ AVC (average variable cost). If P < AVC, the firm shuts down.

## Long-Run Equilibrium

In the long run, economic profits attract entry; losses cause exit. The market reaches a long-run equilibrium where **P = MC = min(ATC)**, and economic profit = 0.

## The Competitive Ideal

Perfect competition is often used as a normative benchmark. Real-world markets deviate from it in various ways — these deviations form the basis for studying imperfect competition and market failures.
